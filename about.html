About Chihuahuas

Small in size, enormous in personality. Here's what you need to know about the world's smallest breed.

The Breed

The Chihuahua is the world's smallest recognised dog breed, yet they carry themselves with the confidence of a dog ten times their size. Originating from Mexico, they are ancient companions with a temperament that blends fierce loyalty, sharp intelligence, and an irresistible charm.

In New Zealand, the breed is recognised by Dogs New Zealand (NZKC) in two varieties: the Smooth Coat and the Long Coat. Both share the same breed standard for conformation, health, and temperament.

What to Look For

When assessing a Chihuahua, whether for show or companionship, the NZKC breed standard looks for:

  • A well-rounded "apple dome" skull
  • Large, luminous eyes full of expression
  • Large, erect ears held alert
  • A compact, slightly longer than tall body
  • A confident, lively, and self-reliant temperament

📏 Breed Standard weight: Not to exceed 2.7 kg (6 lbs). A slightly larger, healthy dog is always preferable to a tiny, unhealthy one.

Training & Socialisation

One of the most important things to understand: Chihuahuas are dogs. They need training, socialising, and mental stimulation just like any larger breed. Skipping this because they're small leads to anxious, reactive behaviour that gives the breed an undeserved reputation.

Prospective owners should expect to invest time in:

  • Puppy socialisation classes (critical before 16 weeks)
  • Basic obedience training
  • Regular exercise (yes, they do need walks!)
  • Patient, consistent house training (small breeds can take longer)

Health Considerations

Chihuahuas are generally hardy, but owners should be aware of these breed-specific conditions:

  • Patella luxation, loose kneecap common in small breeds
  • Heart conditions, monitor with regular vet checks as they age
  • Syringomyelia (SM), neurological condition more common in apple-domed dogs
  • Dental disease, small mouths crowd teeth; budget for regular professional cleans ($300+)
  • Hypoglycaemia, low blood sugar especially in very small puppies

Always ask breeders about health testing and DNA screening. A reputable breeder will have records and be transparent.

Long-term Commitment

Chihuahuas are long-lived companions, with 15 to 20 years not uncommon. Before adding one to your family, consider whether your lifestyle over the next two decades will accommodate a dog: future relationships, children, living situations, and travel plans.
